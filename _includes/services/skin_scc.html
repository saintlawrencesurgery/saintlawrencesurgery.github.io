Squamous cell carcinoma of the skin (SCC)

What is cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma?

Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) is a common type of non-melanoma skin cancer.

Cutaneous SCC is an invasive disease, referring to cancer cells that have grown beyond the epidermis. SCC can sometimes metastasize (spread to distant tissues) and may be fatal.

Who gets cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma?

Risk factors for cutaneous SCC include:

  • SCCs are particularly prevalent in elderly males. However, they also affect females and younger adults.
  • Previous SCC or other skin cancers
  • Actinic keratoses
  • Outdoor occupation or recreation (farming, boating, golfing)
  • Smoking
  • Fair skin, blue eyes and blond or red hair
  • Previous skin injury, thermal burn
  • Genetics
  • Other risk factors include radiation, exposure to arsenic, and immune suppression due to disease.
  • Organ transplant recipients are at great risk for SCC.

What are the clinical features of cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma?

Cutaneous SCCs present as enlarging scaly or crusted lumps..

  • Fast growing
  • They may bleed or ulcerate
  • They may be tender or painful
  • Located on sun-exposed sites, particularly the face, lips, ears, hands, forearms and lower legs

Classification of squamous cell carcinoma by risk

Cutaneous SCC is classified as low-risk or high-risk, depending on the chance of tumor recurrence and metastasis. Characteristics of high-risk SCC include:

High-risk cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma has the following characteristics:

  • Diameter greater than or equal to 2 cm
  • Location on the ear, vermilion of lip, central face, hands, feet, genitalia
  • Arising in elderly or immune suppressed patient
  • Histological thickness greater than 2 mm, poorly differentiated histology, or with invasion of the subcutaneous tissue, nerves and blood vessels

Metastatic SCC is found in regional lymph nodes (80%), lungs, liver, brain, bones and skin.

How is squamous cell carcinoma diagnosed?

Diagnosis of cutaneous SCC is based on clinical features. The diagnosis and histological subtype is confirmed after it is removed so you can tell pathologically by diagnostic biopsy or following excision.

Patients with high-risk SCC may also undergo staging investigations to determine whether it has spread to lymph nodes or elsewhere. These may include:

  • Imaging using ultrasound scan, X-rays, CT scans, MRI scans
  • Lymph node or other tissue biopsy

How can cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma be prevented?

Reducing sun and UV exposure at any time of life reduces the number of SCCs. This is particularly important in ageing, sun-damaged, fair skin; in patients that are immunosuppressed; and in those who already have actinic keratoses (pre-cancers) or previous SCC.

  • Stay indoors or under the shade in the middle of the day
  • Wear covering clothing/li>
  • Apply high protection factor SPF50+ broad-spectrum sunscreens generously to exposed skin if outdoors
  • Avoid indoor tanning (sun beds, solaria)

What is the outlook for SCC?

Most SCCs are cured by treatment especially if found and treated quickly.

Around 50% of people diagnosed with SCC develop a second one within 5 years. They are also at increased risk of other skin cancers, especially melanoma. Regular self-skin examinations and long-term annual skin checks by an experienced health professional are recommended.
