Basal cell carcinoma (BCC):

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is a common, locally invasive, non-melanoma skin cancer.

Who gets basal cell carcinoma?

Risk factors for BCC include:

  • Age and gender: BCCs are particularly prevalent in elderly males. However, they also affect females and younger adults
  • Previous BCC or other form of skin cancer (squamous cell carcinoma, melanoma)
  • Sun damage
  • Repeated prior episodes of sunburn
  • Fair skin, blue eyes and blond or red hairâ€”note; BCC can also affect darker skin types
  • Previous cutaneous injury, thermal burn, disease (ex. cutaneous lupus, sebaceous nevus)
  • Genetics
  • Other risk factors include radiation, exposure to arsenic, and immune suppression due to disease or medicines

What causes basal cell carcinoma?

There are many causes which may include one or all of the following:

  • Genetics
  • Sun or radiation exposure
  • Immunosuppression
  • Spontaneously occur

What are the clinical features of basal cell carcinoma?

BCC is a locally invasive skin tumor. The main characteristics are:

  • Slowly growing plaque or nodule
  • A non-healing lesion which may spontaneously bleed or ulcerate
  • Skin colored, pink or pigmented
  • Can be very small or large

BCC is rarely life threatening. Seldom, BCCs grow rapidly/deeply, and/or spread to local lymph nodes.

Types of basal cell carcinoma

Nodular BCC

  • Most common type of facial BCC
  • Shiny or pearly nodule with a smooth surface
  • May have central depression or ulceration, so its edges appear rolled
  • Blood vessels cross its surface

Superficial BCC

  • The most common type in younger adults
  • Commonly on the chest and shoulders
  • Scaly or an irregular plaque
  • Rolled borders

How is basal cell carcinoma diagnosed?

BCC is diagnosed clinically by the presence of a slowly enlarging skin lesion with typical appearance. The diagnosis and histological subtype is usually confirmed pathologically by a diagnostic biopsy or following excision.

What is the treatment for primary basal cell carcinoma?

The treatment for a BCC depends on its type, size and location, the number to be treated, patient factors, and the preference or expertise of the doctor. Most BCCs are treated surgically. Long-term follow-up is recommended to check for new lesions and recurrence.

Excision biopsy

Excision means the lesion is cut out and the skin stitched up.

  • Should include 3 to 5 mm margin of normal skin around the tumor
  • Lesions are sent to a pathologist to examine for cancer cells and to make sure the borders are clear of cancer
  • Further surgery is recommended for lesions that are incompletely excised

Mohs Excision

Mohs surgery involves examining carefully marked excised tissue under the microscope, layer by layer, to ensure complete excision.

  • Very high cure rates achieved by trained Mohs surgeons
  • Used in high-risk areas of the face around eyes, lips and nose for optimal cosmetic effect
  • Suitable for larger, more complex lesions
  • Large defects are repaired by flap or skin graft

Cryotherapy

Cryotherapy is the treatment of a superficial skin lesion by freezing it with liquid nitrogen.

  • Suitable for small superficial BCCs on covered areas of trunk and limbs
  • Results in a blister that crusts over and heals within several weeks.

How can basal cell carcinoma be prevented?

The most important way to prevent BCC is to avoid excessive sun exposure. This is especially important in childhood and early life. Fair skinned individuals and those with a personal or family history of BCC should protect their skin from sun exposure daily, year-round.

  • Stay indoors or under the shade in the middle of the day
  • Wear covering clothing
  • Apply high protection factor SPF50+ broad-spectrum sunscreens generously to exposed skin if outdoors
  • Avoid indoor tanning (sun beds, solaria)

What is the outlook for BCC?

Most BCCs are cured by treatment, especially when diagnosed and taken care of quickly.

About 50% of people with BCC develop a second one within 3 years of the first. They are also at increased risk of other skin cancers, especially melanoma. Regular self-skin examinations and long-term annual skin checks by an experienced health professional are recommended.
